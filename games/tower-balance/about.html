Tower Balance

A seesaw balancing game where each round presents a colored block labeled with its weight — 1, 2, or 3 units. Press the Left or Right arrow key (or tap the matching button) to add that block to the corresponding side of the scale. The beam tilts based on the accumulated weight difference between the two sides. If the tilt reaches 60 degrees, the seesaw tips over and the game ends. Place as many blocks as you can while keeping both sides roughly equal.

How to Play

  • A colored block with a weight label (1, 2, or 3) appears at the top of the screen
  • Press ◀ Left Arrow or tap the Left button to place it on the left side
  • Press ▶ Right Arrow or tap the Right button to place it on the right side
  • On touch screens, you can also tap the left half or right half of the canvas directly
  • A countdown bar under the block gives you 5 seconds to choose — if it runs out, the block auto-places on the lighter side
  • The beam tilts further with every weight difference; the beam turns red when you are close to the limit
  • Game ends when the tilt exceeds 60 degrees
  • Your score is the total number of blocks placed before the seesaw tipped

About This Game

Tower Balance is a decision-making game built around a simple but surprisingly deep arithmetic problem: given a running imbalance and an unknown future sequence of weights, which side should you add to in order to maximize longevity? The beam tracks the cumulative weight difference between the left and right stacks. A small early imbalance compounds over time if you keep adding to the heavier side — but a block of weight 3 can also instantly correct a gap that took three weight-1 blocks to build up.

The weight values are drawn randomly from the set {1, 2, 3}, which means each new block is either just enough to fix a minor imbalance or a significant swing that forces you to think several blocks ahead. A run of low-weight blocks rewards steady alternation; a cluster of high-weight blocks punishes players who haven't been correcting imbalances proactively.

The 5-second countdown per block creates mild time pressure that prevents over-analysis. In practice most decisions resolve in under a second — the interesting moments are when the left and right totals are close and the incoming weight is large enough to matter either way. These are the decision points where the game earns its depth: adding a 3 to the wrong side with both totals already high is a very different risk than adding a 1 when you have comfortable margin.

Why We Built It

Balance puzzles and weighing games have appeared in logic and math education for centuries because the core concept — two sides of a scale, one correct answer — is instantly understood by anyone. Tower Balance adapts this intuition into a fast-paced arcade game where the "correct answer" changes every round based on the accumulated history. The result is a game that rewards mental arithmetic and foresight without being intimidating: even a player who cannot add quickly can play by feel, placing blocks alternately and relying on the visual beam angle to guide corrections. The visual feedback is honest and immediate — the beam position is always a direct, readable representation of the current weight difference, with no hidden state.

Tips & Strategy

  • Track the running totals — the weight counts displayed under the beam plates tell you the exact imbalance at a glance; use them, not just the tilt angle.
  • Prioritize large corrections over perfect balance — if the left side is 5 heavier and you get a weight-3 block, put it right even if it slightly over-corrects; a small over-correction is easier to fix than a growing deficit.
  • Alternate when weights are equal — a sequence of weight-1 blocks is most safely handled by strict alternation; save your decision-making effort for the weight-3 blocks.
  • Don't let the timer run out — the auto-place rule picks the lighter side, which is usually correct, but you lose the chance to over-correct a growing imbalance on purpose.
  • The beam warning is your last chance — once it turns red you are within two or three large blocks of tipping; start placing every block on the heavy side until the beam color returns to white.

Ideas for Improvement

  • Weights up to 5 — higher weight values as the score climbs, increasing the per-block impact and forcing faster mental arithmetic.
  • Preview queue — show the next two or three incoming block weights so players can plan ahead rather than reacting one block at a time.
  • Strict tighter limit — reduce the maximum angle as a difficulty setting, shrinking the margin for error.
  • Two-player mode — one player controls Left, another controls Right, competing to be the one who does not tip the seesaw.
