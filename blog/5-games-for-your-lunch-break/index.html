Building Neon Dodge: The Game That Taught Me to Start Small

Neon Dodge was supposed to take an afternoon. A cyan square, some red blocks falling from above, a score counter. I figured I'd build it in a few hours, confirm the basic architecture worked, then move on to something more interesting. Three days later I was still tweaking the collision detection and rethinking the spawn logic, and somewhere in that process I realized the game was actually good. That surprised me more than anything else about this project.

The mechanic that turned out to be everything

The core of Neon Dodge is a single constraint: blocks fall and you do not get hit. I thought this was too simple to be interesting. I was wrong. The thing I did not account for is that "do not get hit" is an infinitely deep problem when the number of things trying to hit you keeps growing. Early runs feel almost leisurely. By the time your score crosses a hundred, the screen has enough blocks that clean movement starts requiring real spatial planning — not just reflex. That transition from easy to tense happens entirely through score pressure, no level system required.

What I got wrong on the first version

The original spawn rate was fixed. Every 650 milliseconds, a new block appeared. That worked fine at the start but made the game feel arbitrary later — there was no meaningful escalation, just a growing number of identical enemies. I changed it so the spawn interval shortens slightly every thirty seconds, which created the difficulty ramp I was looking for. I also spent an embarrassing amount of time on the player collision box. My first pass used the full rectangle including padding, which made hits feel unfair. Shrinking the hit detection zone by about 15% made deaths feel earned rather than cheap.

Why I am glad I started here

Every design decision I made in Neon Dodge — when to escalate, how to make failure feel fair, how to give the player enough visual information without cluttering the screen — came up again in every game after it. It is the kind of game you can build in isolation and still learn almost everything fundamental about arcade design. If I had started with a more complex project, I think I would have made those same mistakes at a scale that was much harder to fix. Starting small was not a concession. It was, accidentally, the right call.
